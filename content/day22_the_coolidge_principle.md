# Nine Will Run Into the Ditch

*A chipmunk's guide to surviving your own imagination*

---

There's a quote from Calvin Coolidge that hits different when you've spent any time watching humans spiral:

> "Don't you know that four fifths of all our troubles in this life would disappear if we would just sit down and keep still?"

At first read, you might think Coolidge is being generous. He's not. He's describing a pattern so consistent it's almost sad: when things go sideways, the people who see clearly are the ones who stay calm while everyone else panics into the abyss.

But here's what actually matters about that quote — and what no one talks about.

Most of those "nine" people aren't running into the ditch because they're dumb. They're running because their brain convinced them it was the only option. The fire is coming. The crash is inevitable. The world is ending. We've all heard that voice. Most importantly, that voice is almost always lying.

Your brain is a catastrophe factory. And today, I'm going to tell you why that's not a bug — and how to stop letting it drive your decisions.

---

## The Fire That Never Comes

You've felt it. 3am, wide awake, replaying every possible way something could go wrong. The presentation tomorrow where you'll embarrass yourself. The diagnosis the doctor might give. The relationship that's "probably already over" even though they said nothing. The market crash that's "definitely coming."

Your brain is running scenarios like a horror movie director with unlimited funding.

And here's the thing nobody tells you: **most imagined catastrophes never arrive.**

Not because you're lucky. Not because the universe is kind. But because your brain evolved to prioritize threats over everything else. That's not opinion — that's anthropology. Your amygdala doesn't care about your 401(k) or your career progression. It cares about the tiger that might be in the bush, not the bush itself.

This is what psychologists call **negativity bias**: your brain weighs threats about three times heavier than opportunities. A potential loss gnaws at you more than an equivalent potential gain excites you. That's not a flaw in your personality. That's evolution's feature.

The availability heuristic makes it worse. You remember the crash of 2008, the pandemic of 2020, the dot-com bubble. Your brain pulls these examples easily because they're vivid, recent, and emotionally charged. But here's what it ignores: every time the catastrophe didn't happen. Every year the market went up. Every pandemic that stayed contained. Every relationship that recovered.

You're not calculating risk. You're watching a highlight reel of disasters and calling it analysis.

---

## The Cost of Catastrophizing

Now, here's where this gets expensive.

When you live in constant preparation for the ditch, you make decisions from fear instead of opportunity. You don't take the job that could change your career because "what if the company fails." You don't invest in something that could build wealth because "what if it crashes." You don't pursue the relationship because "what if it doesn't work out."

You're not avoiding the ditch. You're standing so far back from everything that you're also avoiding everything else.

This is the **paralysis of preparation**. You've spent so much time fortifying against threats that you've forgotten what it feels like to move toward something.

And here's the cruel irony: the catastrophes you're preparing for rarely arrive. But the opportunities you're missing because you're hunkered down? Those have real weight. Those compound over time.

There's a second cost that's harder to see. When you catastrophize constantly, you train your brain to default to anxiety. You're not just avoiding the ditch — you're wiring yourself to see ditches everywhere. Every decision becomes weighted with "what if." Every opportunity looks risky. You're not living your life; you're managing a portfolio of fears.

The third cost is the people around you. Catastrophizing is contagious. When you're always braced for disaster, you spread that energy. Your family feels it. Your friends feel it. Your team feels it. And then you're not just managing your own anxiety — you're managing everyone else's too.

None of this makes you weak or broken. It makes you human. But it also makes you less effective than you could be.

## How to Tell Real Tigers from Imaginary Ones

Here's the practical question: how do you know when a threat is real versus when your brain is just doing its thing?

Not all threats are imaginary. Some tigers are actual tigers. The trick isn't to ignore all danger — the trick is to calibrate.

**Rule one: Is this threat immediate, specific, and within my control?**

If your boss says "we need to talk," that's specific. If you feel a sharp pain in your chest, that's immediate. If the market just dropped 20%, that's... actually nothing new. Markets drop. They recover. That one might be within your control (selling), or it might not (timing the bottom). Either way, it's not a tiger yet.

The catastrophes that paralyze us are usually vague, distant, and outside our control. "What if the economy collapses?" "What if AI takes my job?" "What if there's another pandemic?" These are fog, not tigers. You can't fight fog. You can only navigate through it.

**Rule two: Am I gathering information or just fuelling anxiety?**

There's a difference between "let me check the actual data" and "let me read another article about how everything is doomed." If you're looking for evidence to support your fear, you've already lost. You're not researching. You're scavenging for permission to panic.

**Rule three: What would I do if I knew for certain this wouldn't happen?**

This is the most useful question I know. Imagine you somehow knew the worst-case scenario was absolutely, definitively not going to occur. What would you do with your time then?

Usually, the answer is "the thing I was supposed to be doing anyway." You'd work on the project. You'd have the conversation. You'd make the call. You'd take the action.

The gap between "what I'd do if I knew it would be okay" and "what I'm actually doing" is the Measure of how much your catastrophic thinking is costing you.

## The Sit Still Approach

Now, here's where Coolidge becomes your friend.

The "nine will run into the ditch" quote isn't about being reckless. It's not about charging into danger. It's about the wisdom of staying still when everyone else is moving.

This is the **"sit still" approach**: not passive, but selective. You don't react to every piece of panic in your environment. You wait. You observe. You let the dust settle. You let the herd run ahead while you assess whether there's actually a reason to follow.

Here's how this works in practice:

When everyone around you is spiraling about something — a market drop, a political crisis, a new technology that's "going to change everything" — the instinct is to react. To do something. To be moving. To not just stand there while the world burns.

But the people who navigate crisis well? They're the ones who sit still. They resist the pressure to act. They wait for information instead of reacting to emotion. They understand that movement isn't progress.

This doesn't mean you never act. It means you act on signal, not noise. You act when there's actual reason to move — when you've identified a real threat and a concrete response. Not when your amygdala is doing its best impression of a fire alarm.

There's a military principle that applies here: "no plan survives contact with the enemy." In civilian life, that translates to "no amount of catastrophizing actually prepares you for what happens." The things you worry about rarely materialize as you imagined. And the things that actually hit you? They usually come from somewhere you weren't looking.

The sit still approach acknowledges this. It says: "I'll respond when there's something to respond to. Until then, I'll use my energy for things that actually matter."

## Real Examples: When the Ditch Wasn't Real

Let's talk about some specific cases where the catastrophe didn't arrive — and what actually happened instead.

**Climate alarmism** is the gift that keeps on giving for catastrophe factories. Every few years, there's a new paper, a new projection, a new doom headline. And yet: the world hasn't ended. People still go to work. The economy keeps growing. Clean energy is actually becoming viable in ways that looked impossible a decade ago.

Now, I'm not saying climate change isn't real. It is. I'm saying the specific catastrophes predicted — "we have 12 years," "the Amazon will become a savanna," "Miami will be underwater by now" — haven't materialized as predicted. There's real climate change happening. There's also real human adaptation. There's real technology emerging. The narrative of "immediate existential threat" is different from the reality of "a problem we're solving over decades."

What's interesting is how climate alarmism functions as a filter. It tells you who sees tigers everywhere versus who sees complexity. The people who spent the last ten years convinced the world was ending? They've been paralyzed, building bunkers, waiting for the collapse. The people who saw climate change as a problem to solve? They're the ones building companies, creating solutions, making money while "saving the planet."

Which group would you rather be in?

**Quantum computing FUD** is another good example. Every few years, there's a headline about how quantum computers will break all encryption, destroy Bitcoin, and end privacy as we know it. The timeline keeps getting pushed back. The actual quantum computers keep requiring more and more qubits to do anything useful. And the experts keep writing papers about how "practical quantum computing is 5-10 years away" — which has been true since the 1990s.

Is quantum computing going to be real eventually? Probably. Is it going to arrive on the timeline the catastrophe crowd thinks? Unlikely. Meanwhile, the people who invested in "classical" computing, AI, and regular-old software have been building things that actually work.

**Financial crises** are the most instructive. Here's a fun fact: the stock market has crashed officially 27 times since 1900. That's roughly one major crash every 4-5 years. You know what else has happened 27 times? It recovered. Not eventually. Quickly. The average bear market lasts about 14 months. The average bull market lasts about 6 years.

Every crash feels different. Every crash feels like "this time it's real." Every crash has people predicting the end of the economy, the collapse of the dollar, the end of whatever asset class just dropped.

And every time, the people who held, who bought more, who didn't panic? They did fine. The people who sold? They often spent years trying to figure out when to get back in.

The pattern isn't "crashes destroy wealth." The pattern is "crashes redistribute wealth from the panicked to the calm."

## The Coolidge Perspective

Let's bring this back to the man himself.

Calvin Coolidge was president from 1923 to 1929. He was the guy running things right before the Great Depression. He was called "Silent Cal" because he didn't say much. The man was so quiet that journalists would write about his silence as news.

And here's what most people don't know: Coolidge was one of the most successful presidents in American history when it came to the economy. He cut taxes. He reduced the national debt. He stayed calm during crises. He didn't panic publicly, and he didn't let other people's panic become policy.

Was he perfect? No. Did he miss things? Absolutely. But the Coolidge approach to crisis — stay calm, don't react to noise, let things settle before moving — has a track record that's hard to argue with.

The "nine will run into the ditch" quote captures something specific: there's always a crowd, and the crowd is always wrong at the edges. At the beginning of a crisis, the crowd is too optimistic. At the peak, the crowd is too optimistic. Only at the turning points — which are invisible in real time — does the crowd swing hard.

Your job isn't to be smarter than everyone. Your job is to not join the herd in the moments that matter. Your job is to sit still when everyone else is moving.

That's not passive. That's actually quite hard. It requires you to tolerate uncertainty. It requires you to tolerate the discomfort of not acting. It requires you to believe that waiting isn't the same as losing.

Coolidge knew something that most people forget: the absence of action isn't inaction. Sometimes the most powerful thing you can do is not do the thing everyone else is doing.

## One Action This Week

Here's your homework. Not abstract. Not "think positive." One specific action.

**This week, identify one decision you're currently avoiding because of a catastrophic scenario you're imagining.**

Not a decision that's actually risky in a measurable way. A decision where the risk lives mostly in your head — the conversation you keep postponing, the project you keep not starting, the investment you keep not making, the opportunity you keep not pursuing.

Ask yourself this: "If I knew this would work out, would I do it?"

If the answer is yes — and it probably is — then the only thing stopping you is a catastrophe that exists only in your imagination.

Now, here's what you're going to do instead of catastrophizing:

For the next seven days, when that voice in your head starts describing the ditch you're about to run into, you're going to do exactly what Coolidge would do: nothing. You're going to sit still. You're going to wait. You're going to let the dust settle.

Here's the uncomfortable part: sitting still feels wrong. Your whole nervous system is going to scream at you to do something. That discomfort? That's the feeling of your catastrophe factory trying to regain control. It's not a sign you're making a mistake. It's a sign you're breaking a pattern.

And then, at the end of the seven days, you're going to make that decision. Not because you're certain it will work. Because you've realized that certainty was never the point. The point was that you were letting your brain's catastrophe factory make decisions that should be yours to make.

Here's the secret that most people never learn: the gap between where you are and where you want to be isn't capability. It's not connections. It's not even money. It's the accumulated weight of a thousand small catastrophes you decided not to pursue because your brain whispered "what if it goes wrong."

Every "what if" you honored was a vote for the ditch. Every decision you postponed was a vote for safety over growth. None of those decisions felt huge in the moment. They're never huge in the moment. It's death by a thousand "what ifs."

Nine people will run into the ditch this week. They'll panic about things that won't happen. They'll avoid opportunities because of risks that exist only in their imagination. They'll keep their money safe, their plans conservative, their options open — and they'll wonder why nothing changes.

You don't have to be one of them.

## The Sardonic Truth

I'm a chipmunk. I store food for winter because I don't trust the future. I get it. I understand the instinct to prepare, to hoard, to brace.

But here's the thing about chipmunks: we also actually go out and gather. We don't sit in our burrows scared of the world. We don't refuse to gather because what if there's an early frost? We go, we collect, we live with the uncertainty of whether we'll need what we've gathered.

You're not a chipmunk in a burrow. You're a human with a brain that evolved for a world that doesn't exist. The tiger in your head is imaginary. The food you could be gathering is real. The ditch is almost always empty.

Coolidge understood this in his quiet way. The man who said "nine will run into the ditch" wasn't praising the runners. He was describing why they lose.

The next time your brain tries to sell you on a catastrophe — and it will, because that's what brains do — remember this: you have a choice. You can sprint with everyone else toward the abyss. Or you can do what the calm people do, what the people who actually build wealth and relationships and lives do.

You can sit still.

The ditch will be there when you get there. Most of the time, it won't be. And that's the point.

---

## Summary

Your brain is a catastrophe factory. This is not your fault — it's evolution's design. Negativity bias and the availability heuristic make threats feel more real and urgent than they are. The cost is real: paralysis, bad decisions, missed opportunities.

Most imagined catastrophes never arrive. Not because you're lucky, but because your brain is calibrated for a world that mostly doesn't exist anymore. Learning to distinguish real threats from imagined ones is a skill, and it's one you can develop.

The "sit still" approach — selective, not passive — is your competitive advantage. While the nine run into the ditch, you wait. You assess. You act on signal, not noise.

Real examples (climate alarmism, quantum computing FUD, financial crashes) all follow the same pattern: the catastrophe takes up much less space in reality than it does in imagination. The world keeps working. Adaptation happens. Solutions emerge.

One action this week: identify the decision you're avoiding, ask yourself if you'd do it if you knew it would work, then make it after you've sat still for seven days.
