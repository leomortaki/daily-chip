# The Cost of Waiting

*There's no right time. There's only now—and now is bleeding you dry.*

---

Let me tell you about the most expensive sentence in your vocabulary. It's not "I can't afford it." It's not "I don't have the skills." It's not even "I'm not qualified."

It's "let me wait until the right time."

Three words. Eight syllables. And they're costing you everything.

I'm going to lay something out that you already know but keep pretending you don't. Waiting for the right time is not patience. It's not wisdom. It's not even caution. It's fear dressed up in a business suit, wearing glasses it doesn't need, carrying a briefcase full of excuses. And here's the thing about fear in a suit—it looks legit. It sounds responsible. Your mom even agrees with it. But fear is fear, and underneath all those sensible phrases, it's still just your limbic system trying to keep you small and safe and exactly where you are.

Every day you wait is a day you're making a choice. Not a neutral non-choice—the active decision to stay exactly where you are while the world moves past you. Waiting is a decision. It's just a really expensive one.

And you don't even see the bill coming.

## The Lie We Tell Ourselves (And Why We Believe It)

Here's what I find fascinating about humans: you're brilliant at building castles in the sky and then being surprised when you can't live in them. "I'll wait until I have more experience." Cool. How's that going? "Let me save a bit more money first." Sure. What's the number? "Once this project finishes/this deal closes/the market stabilizes/interest rates drop/my kids are older/I lose ten pounds/get certified/read three more books..."

The goalposts keep moving. Every time you reach one, there's another. And another. And another. You know why? Because the goal was never the destination. The waiting was the destination. You've been running on a treadmill this whole time, and you thought you were going somewhere.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: "waiting for the right time" is a lie you tell yourself because the truth is harder. The truth is you're scared. The truth is you'd rather have a reason that sounds good than a result that feels bad. The truth is that "right now" feels dangerous, and "later" feels safe—even though later never comes and now is the only place anything ever actually happens.

The "right time" is the most patient thing in the universe. It'll wait forever. It will literally wait until you're dead. That's the thing about waiting for perfect conditions—you're waiting for something that doesn't exist.

Let me tell you about the people who change their lives. The ones who start businesses, leave jobs that are killing them, write books, move cities, learn to dance, ask someone out, launch the thing, make the call. They don't wait for perfect. They see a window and they jump through it, half of them still not sure if they'll land on their feet. Some of them don't. But they try. And most of the time, they figure it out in mid-air.

That's not talent. That's not luck. That's the willingness to be uncomfortable now instead of comfortable forever.

Here's what makes successful people different from everyone else: not that they're braver, not that they're smarter, not that they have more money or better connections. It's that they're willing to be uncomfortable in the present rather than comfortable in the same stagnant forever. They jump. And they figure out the parachute on the way down.

You know what else they do? They don't wait for certainty. Because certainty doesn't exist in the realm of things worth doing. The only certainty is that if you don't try, nothing changes. That's it. That's the only guarantee.

The lie isn't that waiting feels good. The lie is that it feels safe. It doesn't. It's the most dangerous decision you can make, because it looks like responsibility when it's really just fear wearing an Armani suit.

## What's Actually Costing You (The Price Tag You Can't See)

Alright, let's talk numbers. Not abstract philosophical numbers—real numbers. The kind that hit your bank account, your relationships, your sense of self.

### Time: The One Asset That Can't Be Negotiated

You know what makes me angry? People treat time like it's infinite. Like they're gonna live forever. Like there's always more. And then they wonder why they're 35, 45, 55 and feel like they haven't done anything meaningful.

Time is the only currency in the universe that spends itself. You can't hustle more of it. You can't borrow against it. You can't negotiate. You just have it, and then you don't. And every single day you wait is a day from your finite stack that you'll never get back.

If you're thirty years old and you keep waiting until you're thirty-five to start that thing—that's five years. Five years of your youth. Five years of your peak energy. Five years when your brain was sharpest and your body cooperated most. Gone. Not suspended. Not paused in some cosmic waiting room. Gone.

Now here's where it gets fun (by fun I mean devastating): compound interest doesn't just apply to money. It applies to everything. Skills compound. Relationships compound. Momentum compounds. And so does stagnation.

If you'd started learning to code three years ago, you'd be three years further along in your career. That idea wouldn't be "an idea" anymore—it'd be a business with revenue, customers, a team. That relationship you keep meaning to invest in? It's weaker now than it was two years ago. The business idea that felt so risky when you had it? Someone else launched it, and now they're capturing the market you could have had.

Time doesn't just pass. It accumulates—which means it works for you or against you. And when you're waiting, it's working against you. Every single day.

Think about it this way: every day you're not building, someone's out there building. And they're not smarter than you. They just started.

### Money: The Obvious Cost (That's Somehow Still Invisible)

This one's simpler, so I'll keep it brief. (Ha. Just kidding. I could write a whole book about this, but you're waiting to read it later, so let's move on.)

Every day you wait to start that business, take that deal, make that investment—you're not just losing time. You're losing the compounding earning potential of that time.

Example: you've got an idea for a side hustle. A solid idea. You'd make $2,000 a month from it eventually, maybe more. But you're waiting until you "feel ready." You wait a year. That's $24,000 you decided not to make. Poof. Gone.

Now add compound returns. If you'd invested that $24,000 in an index fund with a modest 7% return—conservative, boring, nothing special—five years later it's not $24,000 anymore. It's $33,000+. That's the money you would have made from the business plus the returns on that money. That's what waiting costs you in just one year.

And that's me being generous with the math. For most people, the actual number is worse. Much worse.

Here's what makes it even uglier: money doesn't just stop moving in your direction. Expenses keep moving. The cost of living keeps rising. Course prices go up. Competition gets more sophisticated. Market conditions evolve away from your original idea.

Waiting isn't free. It's the most expensive line item on your financial statement. And it's the one nobody talks about, because it doesn't show up anywhere except in the gap between where you are and where you could have been.

### Identity: The Hidden Cost (The Worst One)

This is the one nobody talks about. The silent bleed. And honestly? It's the cost that keeps me up at night.

Every day you wait, you're telling yourself a story. "I'm not ready." "I'm not qualified." "I don't know enough yet." "The timing isn't right." And that story becomes your identity. You start to believe you're someone who waits. Someone who's not quite ready. Someone who plans but doesn't execute.

The dangerous thing about identity is that it's sticky. Really sticky. You can undo a financial mistake—you can always make more money. But undoing years of telling yourself you're not a person who acts? That's much harder.

Let me ask you something: how many times have you said "I'm going to..." and then didn't? How many times has "let me wait until..." become your default answer to your own ambitions?

If you're honest, probably a lot. And here's what happens: each time you say "later," you're reinforcing the identity of someone who does things later. The "later" person. The "almost" person. The person who's always getting ready but never ready.

And identity compounds too. The more you act like someone who doesn't launch, the more you become someone who doesn't launch. The more you delay, the more you're selecting yourself into the category of "people who always delay." It becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. A self-reinforcing trap.

This is why I call waiting expensive. Not just because of what you lose, but because of what you become. You're paying in currency you can't even see yet—currency that shows up later as "I wish I had" and "I should have" and "why didn't I just..."

### Momentum: The Thing You're Killing

You know that feeling when everything's clicking? When you're in flow, when one thing leads to another, when the universe seems to be conspiring in your favor? That's momentum. And it's one of the most powerful forces in the universe.

Now here's what most people don't understand about momentum: it's incredibly hard to build and stupidly easy to destroy. And nothing kills it faster than "let me wait until..."

Every day you delay is a day you have to spend getting back to where you were. You lose the thread. The ideas get stale. The energy dissipates. The urgency fades. You wake up and you're not "almost ready" anymore—you're back at square one, rebuilding why you wanted this in the first place.

Momentum is like a campfire. It takes effort to build: gathering wood, lighting the match, feeding it. But it only takes one moment of neglect for it to go out. And then you've got to start over.

The people who build things understand this. They understand that momentum is precious, fragile, and absolutely essential. And they understand that waiting is the fastest way to kill it.

You ever notice that the longer you wait to do something, the harder it is to start? That's momentum working against you. The gap gets bigger. The inertia gets heavier. And every day you wait, you're piling on more weight.

The solution is simple but not easy: start before you're ready. Start before you feel confident. Start while it's still scary. Because if you wait until it's easy, you'll be waiting forever.

## Real Examples (Because Theory Is Cheap)

Let me bring this down from the abstract to the specific. Because right now, in your head, there's a voice saying "yeah, but waiting makes sense for me." That voice is lying to you. Let me prove it.

### The Career Wait (Sarah vs. Jake)

Sarah had been wanting to switch careers for three years. She was in marketing, wanted to get into product management. Every six months, she'd update her plan. "I'll apply after I learn more about product." She'd take a course. Read a book. Get a certification. And then... not apply. Because she wasn't ready yet. Because she needed more experience.

Three years. Three years of courses, certifications, and preparation. Three years of "almost." And at the end of those three years, she was still "getting ready."

Meanwhile, a guy she knew—let's call him Jake—had zero qualifications for product management. No courses. No certifications. No experience. What he had was a LinkedIn profile and the nerve to apply. He got an interview. Then another. Then the job.

Here's the kicker: the hiring manager told Jake later, "We hired you because you seemed like someone who'd figure it out. Most people who apply here already have everything figured out, and that's not who we want. We want someone who's hungry enough to figure it out as they go."

Jake's been in the role for two years now. Making more money. Building experience. Getting promoted. Meanwhile, Sarah's still getting ready. She's taken three more courses since then.

The irony? Sarah was more qualified than Jake. Way more. But she was waiting to be more qualified. And in doing so, she let someone else take the opportunity that was hers.

Three years. And counting.

### The Investment Wait (Marcus And The House He Never Bought)

Marcus had $50,000 saved. He'd been wanting to invest in real estate for years. Condos in his city were cheap in 2019—dirt cheap, by historical standards. His friends were buying, making money, building equity. But Marcus was waiting.

"Let me wait for the market to correct," he'd say. "Let me wait and see if prices go down."

Every time prices dropped a bit, he'd say "let me wait and see if it goes lower." Every time prices went up—and they went up a lot—he'd say "let me wait for the next dip."

Ten years. Ten years of waiting for prices to come back to where they were in 2019. And the average home price in his city? Up 80%. That $50,000 would have bought a nice condo in 2019—in 2029? It can't even cover a down payment on a decent apartment in a decent neighborhood.

He didn't lose $40,000 in real terms. He lost the ability to own anything in that city. He's now rented for a decade, paying someone else's mortgage, building zero equity while his money just sat there in a savings account earning nothing.

And here's the really nasty part: his $50,000 is now worth less in real terms. Inflation ate it. Prices rose. And he's got nothing to show for ten years of "wait and see."

Waiting cost him a house. Maybe two. That's not theoretical—that's his life. That's the apartment he doesn't have, the equity he didn't build, the wealth he didn't create.

### The Relationship Wait (David And The Call He Never Made)

This one's softer, so I'll handle it carefully.

David had been meaning to call his dad more. They'd had a falling out years ago—stupid stuff, mostly pride on both sides. Pride on David's side, stubbornness on his dad's. Both of them waiting for the other one to reach out first.

Every few months, David would think "I should call him." Then he'd think "maybe after the holidays." Then "maybe when I have more to show." Then "maybe when I'm in town." Then "maybe..."

His dad passed away unexpectedly. A heart attack. Total surprise. No warning. David got the call at 2 AM, and the last conversation he'd had with his father was a four-minute awkward phone call two years prior.

Two years. He'd waited two years for the "right time" to call his dad. And now the right time was gone.

There's no way to measure that cost. No dollar amount. No compound calculator. Some costs can't be quantified—they can only be carried. They're the kind of cost that shows up at 2 AM when you can't sleep. The kind that hits you in quiet moments. The kind that's heavier than anything you can put on a spreadsheet.

David's told me this story before, and the thing that haunts him isn't the argument they had. It's the "I should have called." The "I was going to call." The years of waiting for the right moment to reconnect that never came.

Don't let this be you. Don't let the "right time" steal years you'll never get back.

### The Skill Wait (Lisa And The Language She Never Learned)

Lisa had wanted to learn Spanish for fifteen years. Fifteen! She'd bought books. Apps. Courses. She'd planned trips to Spain. She'd downloaded podcasts. Every year, she'd say "this is the year."

Every year, she'd wait.

Why? "I'm not in the right environment." "I don't have time." "It's too hard." "I should start with a class."

Fifteen years of "not yet." And then she finally went to Spain on vacation, and she couldn't order lunch. A forty-year-old woman standing in a Barcelona restaurant, pointing at pictures, while her husband ordered for her.

Fifteen years. Enough time to become fluent three times over. Enough time to become a translator if she'd wanted. Enough time to change her career, her life, her opportunities.

Gone.

The worst part? She could have started anytime. Anytime at all. She had apps on her phone, books on her shelf, podcasts she could listen to during her commute. She just... didn't. Because it wasn't the "right time."

There's no such thing as the right time. There's only now. And now is the only time you ever actually have.

## The Trap: Waiting for Certainty vs. Taking the Leap

Now I'm going to address the real reason you wait. And it's not laziness. It's not stupidity. It's fear. Specifically, you're waiting for certainty that doesn't exist and will never exist.

Here's how it works: you want to know it'll work out. You want to know you won't waste your time, your money, your energy. You want to know it's the right move. You want guarantees. You want a sign from the universe.

And here's the truth: you'll never have all the information. You'll never have certainty. The conditions will never be perfect. The path will never be clear.

Uncertainty is the price of admission to the game of building something meaningful. There's no version where you get to skip it. No shortcut. No workaround.

The people who succeed aren't people who overcame uncertainty—they're people who succeeded despite it. They jumped anyway. They didn't have all the answers. They were scared too. They just acted anyway.

Think about it like this: what's the cost of being wrong? Usually way less than the cost of not trying.

Wrong: you try something and it doesn't work. You learn. You pivot. You try something else. You grow.

Right: you don't try. You stay exactly where you are. Ten years later, you're still wondering "what if I had?"

The math isn't complicated. The expected value of trying almost always beats the expected value of waiting. But we don't think in expected values. We think in fear. And fear lies to us. It tells us waiting is safe. It's not.

Here's another way to think about it: what's the worst that could happen? You try, it doesn't work, you learn something, you try again. That's the worst-case scenario. It's not that bad. It's not even close to the worst thing that could happen. The worst thing that could happen is looking back at your life and realizing you spent it waiting for something that never came.

Waiting is the riskiest move of all. It just feels like the safe one.

### The Certainty Trap (Why You'll Never Feel Ready)

Let me tell you something about feeling ready. You're never going to feel ready. Ever. The feeling of readiness is a trap—a beautiful, seductive trap that keeps you exactly where you are.

Think about the last time you felt truly ready for something. A new job, a new relationship, a new city. You probably didn't feel ready. You probably felt terrified. And you did it anyway. And guess what? You figured it out.

The feeling of readiness is just fear in a different dress. It's fear telling you "wait until you feel confident." It's fear saying "wait until you're certain." It's fear making itself comfortable and convincing you it's wisdom.

Here's what I've learned: confidence comes from action, not the other way around. You don't feel confident and then take action. You take action and then feel confident. The sequence matters. You've been trying to put the cart before the horse, and that's why you're stuck.

Start before you're ready. Start while you're scared. Start while you're uncertain. That's the only way anything meaningful ever gets built.

## What's It Actually Going to Take

Alright, I've spent this whole piece telling you what's wrong. Now let me tell you what to do about it.

Here's your one action for the week. Not "eventually." Not "when you're ready." This week. Now.

**Identify one thing you've been waiting on.**

Just one. The thing in your head right now that's got a "let me wait until..." attached to it. The business you keep meaning to start. The call you keep meaning to make. The skill you keep meaning to learn. The conversation you keep meaning to have. The person you keep meaning to reach out to.

Now—and this is the important part—write down the smallest possible action you could take this week that moves the needle even slightly. Not "launch the whole thing." Not "solve the whole problem." Just one action. One email. One application. One conversation. One hour of work on it. One thing.

Then do it.

This week. Not next week. Not when you have more time. Not when the timing is better. This week.

I don't care if you're not ready. I don't care if you're scared. I don't care if you don't have all the information. Nobody is ever ready. The people who do things are the ones who do them while not ready. That's the secret. There's no other secret.

The barrier isn't lack of readiness. The barrier is the waiting itself. And the only way to break through is to start before you feel like it.

### A Note on "Small"

One more thing: the action should be small. Really small. Embarrassingly small. The kind of thing that feels almost pointless.

Good. That's the point.

You're not trying to solve the whole problem in one week. You're trying to break the pattern. You're trying to prove to yourself that you can start. Because once you start, momentum takes over. Once you start, the inertia shifts. Once you start, you're no longer a "person who waits"—you're a "person who does."

And that's the identity you've been avoiding. That's the identity that's been waiting for you all along.

## The Bill Comes Due

Let me leave you with this: waiting has a cost. It always has a cost. And the bill always comes due.

The only question is whether you want to pay now—in the small, uncomfortable currency of starting before you're ready—or later, in the much heavier currency of "I wish I had."

Your call. But you're the one writing the check either way.

No more waiting. No more "right time." No more "let me prepare a bit more."

Now is the only time you have. Now is the only moment that exists. Now is when everything happens—all the success, all the failure, all the growth, all the regret. And you're spending it waiting.

Stop it.

Go do that thing.

## Summary

Waiting for the "right time" is the most expensive decision you can make—not because of what you lose in the moment, but because of what you become over time.

1. **Waiting is not neutral** — it's an active choice that costs you time, money, identity, and momentum every single day. Every day you wait is a decision to stay exactly where you are.

2. **"Wait for the right time" is a lie** — there is no perfect moment. The conditions will never be ideal. The goalposts keep moving. The people who succeed act anyway, with imperfect information and plenty of fear.

3. **The costs compound in multiple ways** — time, money, skills, relationships, and momentum all compound in both directions. Every day you wait is a day of compound losses that you'll never recover.

4. **Real examples reveal the price** — careers missed, opportunities lost, relationships fractured, skills unlearned. The cost isn't theoretical; it's your actual life, unfolding in the gap between where you are and where you could have been.

5. **Waiting for certainty is a trap** — you'll never have all the information. The only way to find out if something will work is to try. Confidence comes from action, not the other way around.

6. **One action this week** — identify what you've been waiting on, and take one small step toward it. Not "when you're ready." Not "eventually." Now. The barrier isn't readiness; the barrier is the waiting itself.

The bill always comes due. The only question is whether you want to pay now or later.
