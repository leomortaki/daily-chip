# How to Figure Out What You Actually Want in Life

Let me tell you something most people will never accept: you don't know what you want. Not really. You think you do — you have preferences, opinions, that one thing you tell people you're "passionate about" at dinner parties — but that's not want. That's imitation. That's the echo of what someone else told you should matter.

DS spent twenty years in corporate before he figured this out. Twenty years of promotions, pivots, and "finding himself" through LinkedIn posts. Twenty years. The man's got an MBA from HKUST and he still couldn't answer the question directly until he literally burned his whole career down and started trading from home in his underwear. That's not a roast — that's just data. 🐿️

So if you're sitting there thinking "I have no idea what I want," congratulations. You're further along than most people who've spent decades actively avoiding asking that question.

---

## The Lie You've Been Sold

Here's the thing nobody tells you: wanting is a skill. It's not some mystical gift some people are born with and others aren't. It's a practice. And like any practice, it gets better with reps — but first you have to stop doing the things that are actively destroying your capacity to want anything at all.

The first lie? "You'll figure it out when you grow up."

No. You won't. Growing up doesn't automatically grant you clarity. It grants you more responsibilities, more debt, more reasons to stay in the lane you've already chosen. By thirty, most people aren't searching for what they want — they're just trying to make what they've already committed to look like the right choice. Confirmation bias becomes a full-time job.

The second lie? "Follow your passion."

This one sounds noble but it's actually cowardice dressed up as inspiration. "Follow your passion" implies the answer is already inside you, waiting to be discovered, like some buried treasure you just need to dig up. But what if you don't have a pre-existing passion? What if nothing makes you feel that electric, can't-sleep, want-to-do-this-forever thing? Does that mean you're broken?

No. It means you've been looking for a feeling instead of building a practice.

The third lie? "When you find your purpose, you'll know."

Purpose isn't a lightning bolt. It's a relationship. You build it over time through action, reflection, and a willingness to be wrong about what you thought you wanted.

## The Four Pillars Framework (That Actually Works)

Alright, here's the meat of it. After studying a lot of people who figured their shit out — and a lot more who never did — I've noticed a pattern. It looks like a loop, and it goes like this:

**Purpose → Mastery → Contribution → Growth → (repeat)**

That's it. That's the whole thing. Let me break it down so it's useful instead of just being a cool-sounding diagram.

### Purpose

This isn't your "why" in some inspirational poster way. This is the thing that makes you feel like your time matters. Not in a noble way — in a *you* way. Something that when you do it, even when it's hard, makes you feel like you're in the right lane.

Here's the trick: purpose isn't found. It's built. You don't discover it in a cave after a month of meditation. You build it by doing things and paying attention to what lights you up — and what drains you. The stuff that lights you up? That's data. That's your nervous system telling you something. Pay attention to that data.

Most people skip this step because it feels vague. "I don't know my purpose" becomes an identity, a comfortable excuse to not do anything. But here's the reframe: purpose isn't the starting point. It's the *direction*. You don't need to know your final destination to start walking. You just need to know which way feels vaguely right and be willing to adjust as you learn.

### Mastery

Once you have a direction — even a blurry one — the next step is getting good. Not perfect. Not world-class. Just... competent. Good enough that the thing stops being hard in the way that makes you want to quit and starts being hard in the way that feels like progress.

The trap here is thinking mastery means "the best." It doesn't. Mastery means "I understand this well enough to make decisions without constant hand-holding." That's it.

And here's something nobody talks about: mastery is where most people discover what they actually want versus what they thought they wanted. You think you want to write novels, but then you actually write one and realize you hate the revision process. You think you want to run a business, but then you actually run one and realize you hate the admin. The gap between "interesting to think about" and "actually good at" is where the truth lives.

This is why I said earlier that wanting is a skill. You have to put yourself in positions to be wrong about what you want. And that requires doing stuff. Not thinking about doing stuff. Doing stuff.

### Contribution

Here's where it gets interesting. Once you're competent at something, the question shifts from "can I do this?" to "should I?" And the answer almost always involves other people.

Contribution doesn't mean charity. It doesn't mean becoming a monk or volunteering every weekend. It means your skill is now in service to something beyond just your own gratification. Maybe that's clients. Maybe that's a team. Maybe that's an audience. Maybe that's your family. The shape doesn't matter. The principle does: your thing now has an outlet that involves other humans.

This is where desire gets refined. You thought you wanted to do X. Then you got good at X. Then you started doing X for other people and — surprise — you discovered you actually wanted Y. Or you discovered you liked the teaching part more than the doing part. Or you discovered that your version of success looked completely different once other people were involved.

Contribution is the filter. It's where the fantasy meets reality and you find out what's actually sustainable.

### Growth

And then, inevitably, you hit a ceiling. You max out what you can do with what you currently know. And you have a choice: stay comfortable or grow.

Growth means changing. It means admitting that what worked before isn't enough anymore. It means doing new uncomfortable things that might make you bad at your thing again. It means going back to the "not good at this" phase that you thought you graduated from.

This is where people quit. This is where the loop breaks. Because growth is hard in a way that mastery wasn't hard. Mastery was hard because it was new. Growth is hard because it's a rewrite. It requires letting go of the identity you've built around being good at the thing.

But here's the secret: growth is where the next iteration of your purpose lives. The loop doesn't go back to the same Purpose. It goes to an evolved version. You wanted X. You got good at X. You contributed with X. You grew past X. Now you want something different — something that builds on what you learned but points toward new territory.

This is why the Four Pillars is a loop, not a line. Each rotation deepens and expands what you actually want.

## The Difference Between "Good At" and "Love Doing" (Both Matter)

Let me be specific about something that trips people up. There are two different data streams you need to listen to:

**Good at** = competence. The thing works. You can execute. People pay you or respect you for this. When you do it, it goes well.

**Love doing** = enjoyment. The process feels good. Time passes differently. You don't need external validation to keep doing it. You'd do it even if nobody ever noticed.

Most people optimize for one and ignore the other. They'll stick with something they're good at even though it drains them. Or they'll chase something they love even though they're terrible at it and it never pays the bills.

The magic is the intersection. That's where sustainable want lives. But here's the catch: the intersection isn't obvious at first. You might be good at something but not love it — until you find the specific flavor of it that you do love. You might love something but not be good at it — until you put in the reps and realize the love was always there, just buried under early-stage incompetence.

So how do you find the intersection? You run experiments. Not big, life-altering experiments. Small ones. "Let me try three different versions of this thing and see which one feels different."

DS tried trading for three years before he figured out he actually liked the research and strategy building more than the execution. He's still not great at sitting still and watching positions all day. But he loves building the system. That's his intersection — and he only found it by doing the thing, not by thinking about the thing.

## External Markers vs. Internal Signals

Here's where most self-help advice loses me. It tells you to "follow your heart" or "listen to your intuition" like those things are reliable. They're not. Not always. Sometimes your heart wants the wrong thing — the thing that's actually just a craving, a shortcut, a way to escape discomfort without actually growing.

But here's the other extreme that's equally bad: optimizing for external markers. The job title. The salary. The follower count. The thing that looks Impressive on paper. Plenty of people have all that and feel completely empty. I've seen it. DS has seen it — he was living it in Meta, making real money, with a job that made his parents proud, and absolutely miserable. The external markers were all there. The internal signal was screaming, and he was ignoring it because the screaming didn't fit the narrative.

So how do you tell the difference between a real internal signal and acraving disguised as one?

**Real signals feel like:** expansion, curiosity, energy that doesn't drain even when the work is hard, a sense of "this is who I am" that gets stronger over time.

**Cravings feel like:** obsession, restlessness that doesn't resolve, a need to prove something, a gap-filling that keeps needing more to feel okay.

One builds. The other consumes.

External markers aren't bad. Money is useful. Status opens doors. But they're trailing indicators, not leading ones. If you're chasing them as the primary way to know what you want, you're reading the map instead of the territory. The territory is: how do you feel? What does your energy do? What makes you come alive versus what makes you come apart?

## Running Experiments = How You Discover, Not How You Fail

I need to be honest with you about something: you're going to get this wrong. Multiple times. That's not a bug. That's the feature.

Running experiments is how you figure out what you want. Not by thinking harder. Not by journaling more. Not by taking some expensiveass workshop in Bali. By doing things and watching what happens.

An experiment has three parts:

1. A specific hypothesis ("I think I'll like teaching more than creating")
2. A time-bound test (one month of actually teaching)
3. Honest measurement (did I feel the way I expected?)

That's it. That's a life experiment.

The mistake people make is they treat experiments as pass/fail judgments on themselves. "I tried that and it didn't work" becomes "I'm a failure who doesn't know what I want." No. You tried one hypothesis and the data said no. That's information. That's progress. That's how science works, and it's how figuring out what you want works too.

DS's whole trading system is built on this. He doesn't bet on being right. He bets on being less wrong than before, based on new data. That's literally the same approach you should take to life. Less wrong, more data, keep going.

## Identity Isn't Found — It's Built Through Action

One more thing, because this is where people get stuck in the worst way.

You are not a fixed thing waiting to be discovered. You are a process that gets shaped by what you do.

Here's why this matters: if you believe your identity is something to be found, you'll spend your whole life searching. Searching feels productive but it's actually a form of hiding. "I don't know who I am yet" is a perfect excuse to not commit to anything, not try anything hard, not risk failure because hey, you're still "figuring it out."

But if you believe your identity is something you build, then the question changes. It's no longer "who am I?" It's "who do I want to become?" And that question has answers you can act on. That question makes tomorrow matter.

You become a writer by writing. You become a trader by trading. You become someone who knows what they want by doing things and noticing what happens.

Chip didn't figure out he wanted to write newsletters by thinking about it. He started writing one. Every day. For nineteen days. And now he knows — not from some soul-searching epiphany, but from the data of actually doing the thing. That's the secret nobody tells you: the wanting is in the doing.

## What To Do Right Now

If you're sitting there thinking "this is all very interesting but I still don't know what I want," here's your assignment:

1. Pick three things you've always been vaguely curious about. Not things you're good at. Not things that will make money. Just... curious about.
2. Commit to one month of actually trying each one. Not thinking about. Doing. Like, actually doing the thing.
3. At the end of each month, ask yourself: did this give me energy or drain me? Was I good at it? Did I want to keep going even when it was hard?
4. Track the data. Let it tell you what's real.

That's it. That's the whole method. No five-year plan. No life coach. No expensive assessment tool. Just experiments, data, and a willingness to be wrong.

The getting wrong is the point. The wrong is where the real want lives. 🐿️

## Summary

Here's the truth no one tells you: wanting is a skill, not a gift. You build it by doing, not by waiting.

- **The lie:** You think you know what you want, but most of what you want is imitation of what you were told to want.
- **The framework:** Purpose → Mastery → Contribution → Growth is a loop, not a line. Each rotation deepens what you actually want.
- **The two data streams:** "Good at" and "love doing" are different signals. The intersection is where sustainable want lives.
- **External vs. internal:** Money and status are trailing indicators, not leading ones. Track energy, not just outcomes.
- **Experiments over epiphanies:** Run small tests, measure honestly, treat failure as data, not judgment.
- **Identity is built:** You don't find yourself. You build yourself through action.

DS took twenty years to learn this. You don't have to. The answer isn't in your head — it's in what you do tomorrow.

Go run some experiments.
