# Mental Models — How to Think Better Than 99% of People

---

Let me tell you something, friend. Most people think. They think all day, every day. They're thinking while they shower, thinking while they commute, thinking while they scroll through their phones at 2 AM when they should be sleeping. And yet—and here's the uncomfortable truth—most of them are absolutely terrible at it.

Not because they're dumb. Most people aren't dumb. They're smart, actually. Brilliant, in many cases. Some of them have PhDs from fancy schools. Some of them run companies. Some of them manage portfolios that make your head spin. But intelligence without thinking tools is like having a Ferrari with no steering wheel. You're sitting on all this potential and you can't direct it anywhere useful.

That's what mental models are. Steering wheels for your brain.

## What Actually Is a Mental Model (And Why You Should Give a Damn)

A mental model is simply a framework—a lens through which you see, interpret, and navigate the world. It's a conceptual tool that helps you make sense of complexity. Every mental model is a shortcut. Not the lazy kind, the smart kind. The kind that lets you skip 10,000 hours of reinventing the wheel because someone smarter than you already figured out the pattern.

Here's the thing: most people don't even know they have a lens. They think they're seeing reality as it is. They're not. They're seeing reality through a lens they inherited blindly—childhood conditioning, cultural default, whatever their parents/friends/society handed them without explanation. That lens might be helpful. It might be completely broken. They'll never know because they've never examined it.

Mental models are about taking control of that lens. Choosing it deliberately. Understanding its shape so you can swap it out when the situation calls for something different.

Let me give you an example. The ancient Greeks had this model called "First Principles Thinking." The idea is: when you're faced with a problem, don't compare it to similar past problems. Don't ask "what's been done before?" Instead, ask "what's actually true here? What are the fundamental building blocks?" You break everything down to basics and rebuild from there.

Elon Musk uses this. That's why he could look at the space industry and go "wait, why the hell does a rocket cost $300 million? It's made of aluminum and oxygen. What if we just... didn't make it expensive?" That's First Principles Thinking in action. Not comparing. Decomposing. Rebuilding. The same thinking applies to batteries. The same thinking applies to tunnels. It's one model applied across domains.

That's one model. There are hundreds. Some work better in certain terrains. The secret—the thing nobody tells you—is that you don't need all of them. You need maybe 20 that cover 80% of life's situations. But more on that later.

## The Different Types of Mental Models (Know Which One to Reach For)

Mental models aren't all the same. They're like tools—you don't use a hammer for everything. Here's how they break down:

**Reasoning Models** help you think about problems logically. First Principles. Inversion. Deductive and inductive logic. These are for when you need to figure out what's true.

**Systems Models** help you understand how things interact and feedback. Feedback loops. Emergence. Compounding. These are for complex situations where one action triggers cascading effects.

**Decision Models** help you make better choices under uncertainty. Circle of Competence. Pre-Mortems. Reversibility tests. These are for when you need to commit to a direction.

**Probability Models** help you think about likelihood and risk. Normal distribution thinking. Bayesian updating. Margins of safety. These are for risk assessment.

**Interpersonal Models** help you understand human behavior. Incentives. reciprocity. Social proof. These are for understanding why people do what they do.

Understanding which category your mental model belongs to helps you reach for the right one. Otherwise you're just grabbing random tools and hoping something fits.

## A Brief History: Where This Stuff Comes From

The formal study of mental models—the academic version—traces back to a few places. The cognitive scientists Kenneth Craik in the 1940s proposed that the mind builds "miniature representations" of external reality to reason through problems. He was right, but he died too young and nobody really picked up his torch until decades later.

Then the philosophers got involved. Wittgenstein said some things that mattered—about language and how words map to reality—and it boggle the mind that people still get paid to interpret him endlessly. Then the AI researchers in the 70s and 80s—Marvin Minsky, Roger Schank—started building these concepts into actual programs. They were trying to get machines to think. What they learned taught us a lot about how humans think.

But here's what the academics don't tell you: people have been using mental models since the beginning of language. The Bible is full of them—parables are models. Sun Tzu's Art of War? That's a mental model disguised as military strategy. Every proverb, every idiom, every "life lesson" your grandmother told you—that's a mental model, dressed up in folk wisdom. "A stitch in time saves nine."That's systems thinking in two sentences. "Don't put all your eggs in one basket." That's diversification, which is a probability model. Folk wisdom understood mental models before anyone had a name for them.

The business world discovered them in the 90s. Charlie Munger—Warren Buffett's partner at Berkshire Hathaway—made them famous in investing circles. He gave a famous speech at Harvard Law School in 1995 called "The Psychology of Human Misjudgment." If you haven't read it, stop after this newsletter and Google it. It's one of the most practical things ever written about human thinking. Munger went through 24 mental models related to human misjudgment—things like reciprocity, social proof, contrast bias, consistency, scarcity, authority, liking, unity, and more. He made the case that you needed a "latticework of mental models" to invest successfully, which is really just saying you need multiple thinking tools because any single one will fail you in some situations.

Around the same time, the concept of "frameworks" spread through business schools. Porter's Five Forces. SWOT analysis. The BCG Matrix. Some of them useful, some of them corporate poetry that consultants use to justify hourly fees. But the underlying principle was right: having thinking tools beats winging it. The mediocre consultants knew this. The good ones lived it.

The internet accelerated everything. Now you can find mental models for everything from parenting to trading to relationships. Some of it is brilliant. Most of it is rehashed garbage with new fonts and different section headers. But the good stuff is out there if you're willing to look. Shane Parrish at Farnam Street has done more to popularize this than anyone in the last decade. Navalmanack, Tim Ferriss, James Clear—they've all pulled from the same well. The ideas are spreading. The question is whether you're catching them or just watching them float by.

## How to Actually Use These Things (Without Getting Stuck in Your Head)

Here's where most people fail. They read about mental models and suddenly want to apply all of them to everything. They'll face a simple problem—"should I take this coffee meeting?"—and suddenly they're in their head trying to figure out if they should use First Principles, Systems Thinking, Inversion, Circle of Competence, or maybe Second-Order Thinking. Meanwhile, the problem solves itself or gets worse while they're in their head calculating the wrong thing.

Don't do that. That's not thinking. That's analysis paralysis with better branding.

Here's the practical approach:

**1. Build your toolkit slowly. Start with five.**

Pick five mental models that cover different types of problems. Here's a starter set:

- **First Principles Thinking** — For novel problems where you have no reference points. Break it down. Rebuild from truth. Ask "what do I know for certain?" Not what you assume. What you know.
- **Inversion** — For when things aren't working. Instead of "how do I succeed?", ask "how do I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things. This is basically Charlie Munger's idea: "I invert, always invert." He swears by it.
- **Circle of Competence** — For decision-making about new areas. Know what you actually know vs. what you think you know. Stay in your ring. And if you need to expand your ring, do that deliberately—not by fake-it-till-you-make-it but by putting in reps.
- **The Map Is Not The Territory** — For when models become dogma. The model is useful, but it's not reality. Adjust when evidence contradicts. This is the meta-model—knowing that all models are incomplete.
- **Second-Order Thinking** — For when first-order solutions create bigger problems. Ask "and then what?" Ask it again. And again. Most people stop at what they're going to do. Second-order thinkers stop at what happens after the thing they do happens.

That's five. You can add more as you encounter situations that don't fit, but don't rush. Don't be the person with 50 tools and mastery of zero.

**2. Match the model to the problem type, not the problem itself.**

Different problem shapes call for different tools:

- Novel problems with no past reference → First Principles
- Stuck problems that won't budge → Inversion  
- Complex systems with many moving parts → Systems Thinking
- Decisions under uncertainty → Probabilistic Thinking (Bayesian updating)
- Interpersonal conflicts or negotiations → Incentives Thinking (what's driving this person? What's in it for them?)
- Long-term planning → 10/10/10 (how will I feel about this in 10 minutes? 10 months? 10 years? This alone will save you from 80% of dumb short-term decisions)
- Risk assessment → Gaussian Thinking (most outcomes cluster in the middle—plan for that, but don't ignore the tails)
- Strategic planning → Pre-Mortems (imagine it failed—now work backward and defend against those failure modes)

Don't force a model. Let the problem tell you which one fits. If you're forcing it, you're probably wrong.

**3. Practice outside your comfort zone.**

Once you learn a mental model, you won't use it naturally for months. Your brain defaults to whatever it used before—the path of least resistance. That's normal. The trick is to force yourself to apply the model during low-stakes situations until it becomes reflex. You fumble. You forget. You feel stupid. That's the process. Everyone goes through it.

Actually, here's a fun exercise: pick one decision you face this week—it can be as small as "should I take this coffee meeting?"—and explicitly ask yourself "which mental model fits here?" Then apply it. You won't always pick the right model. But you're training the muscle. The gap between "knowing about" and "knowing how to use" is practice. Thousands of reps.

## The Pitfalls (Where Most People Blow It)

Let me save you some pain. Here are the ways people fuck up with mental models:

**1. Mistaking the map for the territory.**

This is the big one. They learn "Systems Thinking" or "Inversion" and suddenly see it everywhere. They won't shut up about feedback loops and second-order effects. They apply it to situations where it doesn't belong. "Oh, that's just a feedback loop," they say, about something that has nothing to do with systems. The model becomes a hammer and everything looks like a nail.

Remember: the model is a tool. You're the wielder. If the tool is damaging more than it's helping, put it down. No one ever got smarter by forcing a framework onto a problem that doesn't need one.

**2. Collecting instead of applying.**

They're the same people who have 47 browser bookmarks, 23 course enrollments, and 14 note apps, and they still feel behind. They think "knowing about" a mental model is the same as "knowing how to use" one. It's not. Knowing about a thing and using a thing are different skills. The gap between them is practice. Thousands of reps. Reading about the swimming strokes won't keep you from drowning—and neither will reading about mental models keep you from making dumb decisions.

Pick five. Master five. That's better than 50 you've glanced at once. Would you rather have five tools you can use or 50 you've bookmarked?

**3. Using them to justify already-established opinions.**

This is the sneakiest one. They already have a conclusion they want to believe—"crypto is a scam," or "this relationship won't work," or "I should quit my job." Then they reach for a mental model to make the conclusion feel rational. "First Principles, you know... when you break it down, crypto has no intrinsic value." That's not First Principles Thinking. That's rationalization with extra steps—the worst kind.

Good mental model use starts with genuine curiosity. You don't know the answer yet. The model helps you find it. If you're already sure of the answer—or worse, emotionally committed to it—you're not thinking. You're just looking for ammunition to shoot down anyone who disagrees.

**4. Ignoring the feedback loop.**

People learn a mental model, apply it once or twice, and if it doesn't work perfectly, they abandon it. "That doesn't work," they say. "It's not practical." That's not how this works. Mental models are approximations. They're wrong sometimes. That's not a bug—it's a feature. You calibrate. You adjust. You try again.

The people who get value from mental models are the ones who treat their thinking like a trading system—track what's working, what's not, and evolve. They have a journal. They note the decisions they've made and why. They review. They update. Most people don't do this because it sounds like homework, which is exactly why most people don't get better at thinking.

**5. Using them as a substitute for action.**

Let me be direct: you're not going to mental-model your way to financial freedom. You're not going to think-your-way to a six-pack. You're not going to logic-your-way into a relationship. Thinking tools help you make better decisions, but they don't make decisions. You do. Thinking is the starting point, not the destination.

Some people get so caught up in optimizing their mental model practice that they never actually do anything. They're always preparing. They're always learning. They're always "building their toolkit." And meanwhile, they're years into this process with nothing to show for it. At some point, you have to actually trade. Or start the business. Or make the ask. The model is there to help you make the decision, not avoid it.

## The Most Useful Mental Models (The Short List)

Let me give you the ones that have genuinely changed how I see problems. You don't need all of them. Just pick the ones that make you uncomfortable—because that usually means they're challenging something you take for granted:

**First Principles Thinking.** Decompose. Rebuild. The classic. Use when you're reinventing something or facing a truly novel challenge where past experience doesn't apply. Tesla didn't become Tesla by asking "what have other car companies done?" It asked "what's a car actually made of? What does it actually need to do?"

**Inversion.** Flip the problem. Ask how you'd fail. Then don't do that. Surprisingly powerful for problems that feel stuck. Can't figure out how to succeed? Figure out how to fail and remove those paths. Charlie Munger's entire approach in one sentence.

**Circle of Competence.** Know your boundaries. Be honest about what you actually understand vs. what you've heard others talk about. Stay in your lane unless you're actively expanding. This saves you from the classic "I got destroyed in an area I thought I understood" moment.

**The Map Is Not The Territory.** All models are imperfect. The question is whether the imperfection matters for the decision at hand. Your SWOT analysis is not reality. Your financial model is not reality. The map helps you navigate—but you're not there yet.

**Second-Order Thinking.** Go one level deeper. And then another. Ask "and then what?" until you run out of gas. This is how you catch the obvious thing everyone else misses. First-order thinkers stop at consequences. Second-order thinkers trace the chain.

**Gaussian Thinking (Normal Distribution Thinking).** Most stuff clusters in the middle. Extreme outcomes are more rare than people assume—but also more impactful than people plan for. Useful for risk assessment, especially when you're tempted to chase extreme upside while ignoring extreme downside.

**Pre-Mortems.** Before starting a project, imagine it failed. What happened? Work backward. Now you have a defensive plan. Gary Klein invented this—the US military uses it, smart companies use it. It's counterintuitive but powerful: instead of visualizing success, you visualize failure. Then you build the antibodies.

**Hanlon's Razor.** "Never attribute to malice that which is adequately explained by stupidity." Or more broadly: assume the simplest explanation first. Most people's problems aren't conspiracies—they're just mess. People aren't out to get you. They're just optimizing for themselves in ways that happen to conflict with you.

**Reversibility (Irreversibility Test).** Before committing, ask: can I undo this? If yes, move faster. If no, slow down. This one alone could save you millions in regret. Many decisions in life are reversible—career changes, investments with exits, relationship situations. Identifying the reversible ones lets you act decisively without infinite exposure.

**The Two-Door Policy.** Life is constantly giving you information. If a decision feels wrong or new evidence comes in, are you the type who stays in the dying marriage/job/industry because you've already invested years? Or do you walk through the door? The ability to course-correct is one of the most underrated skills in success.

## Key Takeaways (What You Should Actually Remember)

1. **Thinking is a skill.** Like any skill, it can be trained. Mental models are your training equipment. Without them, you're just reacting to whatever hits you next. With them, you're responding—which is a choice. The difference between reaction and response is the difference between a leaf in the wind and a sailor reading the currents.

2. **Pick five. Master five.** Start with First Principles, Inversion, Circle of Competence, Second-Order Thinking, and The Map Is Not The Territory. Use them in low-stakes situations first. Let them become automatic before you add more. Collecting without applying is just sophisticated procrastination.

3. **Match the model to the problem.** Not every problem needs every tool. Trying to use Inversion to figure out a novel innovation is wrong inversion. Trying to use First Principles to decide whether to stay in a relationship is wrong framework. Know what each model does.

4. **Stay humble. The model is never the territory.** All models are incomplete. Reality will always have details that your model doesn't capture. If evidence contradicts the model, believe the evidence. Update. Adjust. That's what thinking well looks like in practice.

5. **Stop reading. Start doing.** You're not going to mental-model your way to anything. You're going to do it by making decisions, observing outcomes, and updating your thinking. That's the practice. Everything else is preparation. At some point, you have to actually act.
