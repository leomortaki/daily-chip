# What 'Good Enough' Actually Costs You

*The hidden price of settling is way higher than you think.*

---

You're scrolling through Options B. It's fine. Not great, not terrible. Fine. Your thumb hovers over the order button and some tiny voice in your head whispers, "Eh, good enough."

That phrase — *good enough* — is the most expensive sentence you'll utter this week.

I'm not kidding. You wanna know why most people never build the life they actually want? It's not lack of discipline. It's not bad luck. It's the slow, invisible bleed of settling for "good enough" on things that deserved way more from them. And here's the part that nobody tells you: **the cost of good enough is never lower than the cost of great. It's always higher.** Just not immediately visible.

Let me explain.

## The Lie You Tell Yourself

We like to think of ourselves as sophisticated decision-makers. We run cost-benefit analyses in our heads, weigh trade-offs, calculate expected value. And in that mental spreadsheet, "good enough" often wins because it looks cheaper. Lower upfront cost. Less time. Less effort. Less risk of failure.

But that's because you're only looking at the first-order cost.

The accountant in your head is doing single-entry bookkeeping when it should be doing double-entry. Because here's what good enough actually costs you:

**It costs you the compounding.** Every time you settle, you're not making a one-time trade. You're establishing a baseline. You're teaching your future self what to expect. And then you build on top of that baseline — which means every subsequent compromise happens against the backdrop of your previous compromise.

Your first apartment is "good enough." Then you normalize it. Then you build your social life around its limitations. Then you stay in a job that's "good enough." Then you date someone who's "good enough." Then one day you're 47, sitting in a cubicle that smells like microwave fish, wondering how the hell you got here.

The answer: a thousand small settlements that each felt reasonable in isolation.

## The Counterintuitive Math

Here's where most people get it wrong: They think aiming for the best is the expensive choice. More effort. More time. More resources. Risk of failure.

But let me show you the actual math.

Say you have two paths:

**Path A — Good Enough:** You spend 80 hours on something and get 80% of the results. It costs you less upfront. Feels efficient. Smart, even.

**Path B — Great:** You spend 120 hours on something and get 95% of the results. The marginal 20 hours feel "wasteful" because you've already captured most of the value.

Most people choose A. And from a pure cost-per-point standpoint, they're right — A looks better. 80 hours for 80 points = 1 point per hour. B is 120 hours for 95 points = 0.79 points per hour. Path A wins on efficiency.

Except that's not how value works in the real world. Because outcomes aren't linear. They're threshold-dependent. They're compounding.

The first 80% might get you 80% of the initial value. But the next 15% — the difference between good enough and great — is where the leverage lives. That's where opportunities unlock. That's where relationships transform. That's where you go from "another person who tried" to "the person who actually did it."

The last 15% is worth more than the first 80%.

This is why "good enough" is a trap. You're not optimizing for total value. You're optimizing for upfront cost. And upfront cost is a terrible proxy for total value.

## The SettlingAccount

I want to give you a mental model. It's called the SettlingAccount, and it's the most important spreadsheet you should start keeping.

Every time you settle for "good enough," deposit a point. Not the good kind of point. The bad kind. The kind that compounds against you.

Here's how it works:

**The Rule of Threes:** Most areas of life have three critical thresholds. In your career: employed, senior, or leadership. In relationships: stranger, friend, or partner. In health: alive, functional, or optimal.

Good enough tends to land you one threshold below where you could have been. Not dramatically below. Just one step. Just enough that you don't notice immediately. Just enough that you can explain it away.

But here's the catch: the thresholds aren't evenly spaced. The gap between "good enough" and "great" in most domains is where the real money is. Where the real opportunities are. Where the real relationships are.

When you settle, you're essentially choosing to stop one rung before the ladder actually gets you somewhere.

**The Interest Rate:** Your SettlingAccount doesn't just accumulate linearly. It compounds. Every settlement makes the next settlement easier. If you settled on your last three decisions, your fourth decision will be 10% more likely to settle. Not because you've become a worse person, but because your threshold for "acceptable" has quietly shifted.

This is why people look back at their 20-year-old selves and think, "What was I thinking?" They weren't stupid. They just had a different SettlingAccount balance. And after years of small deposits, the interest kicked in.

## The Examples Nobody Talks About

Okay, enough theory. Let me give you some real ones.

**The Job:** You take a role that's "good enough." The salary is fine. The work is fine. The company is fine. Three years later, you're making 15% more but doing the same work, and you've learned nothing new, and your skills are becoming obsolete, and you can't actually afford the life you want.

Meanwhile, your friend who held out for a slightly worse immediate offer but better learning opportunity? She's now running the team you want to be on. Because she chose the compound. You chose the cash.

The difference wasn't talent. It was whether they optimized for the first year or the seventh.

**The Relationship:** You date someone who's "good enough." They're not terrible. The spark isn't there, but that's okay — relationships are about compatibility, not fireworks, right? You've seen your friends' relationships, and this is... fine.

Five years later, you're married. Two kids. A mortgage. And you're reading this thinking, "Wait, is this what settling looks like?"

I'm not saying you can't build love over time. I'm saying you should start with the raw material for love, not a compromise that you're hoping will grow into something it doesn't have the DNA for.

**The Body:** You work out "good enough." Three times a week. Not four. Not the hard sessions. Just enough to feel like you're doing something. Just enough to not feel guilty.

Ten years later, you're still "working out good enough." You've been on the same fitness plateau for a decade. Your back hurts. Your knees hurt. You can't play with your kids the way you want to.

Meanwhile, the people who went hard for two years and built the habit? They're maintaining now. They built the infrastructure. You're still on level one.

The time investment is the same. The outcome is completely different. Because one optimized for compounding and one optimized for comfort.

## The Trap to Avoid

Here's the trap: **Good enough feels like wisdom.**

It dresses up as pragmatism. As realistic expectations. As not being overly ambitious. "I'm not trying to be a millionaire." "I'm not trying to find my soulmate." "I'm not trying to be the best."

And that's the genius of the trap — it makes you feel self-aware. It makes you feel like you've transcended the naive ambition of people who "don't know how the world works."

But let me tell you what good enough actually is: it's not wisdom. It's fear wearing a sensible outfit.

The person who says "good enough" is often the person who's afraid to try and fail. Or who's afraid to want something badly and not get it. Or who's afraid that wanting more would expose them to judgment.

So they settle. Strategically. Deliberately. With a whole spreadsheet of reasons why this is the rational choice.

And they never notice that the spreadsheet is missing the most important column: **the cost of not trying.**

## One Thing This Week

Here's your homework. Not "think about it." Not "reflect on your patterns."

One concrete action:

**Audit your SettlingAccount.** Find three areas where you've settled for good enough in the last 90 days. Just three. And for each one, ask yourself: What's the actual long-term cost? Not the immediate savings — the long-term cost. The compound cost.

Then ask: What's one thing I could do in the next 7 days that's not good enough — that's actually aimed at great?

It doesn't have to be perfect. It doesn't have to be dramatic. But it has to be a deliberate choice to stop optimizing for the first year at the expense of the seventh.
